Habitats

Biotic(living) and Abiotic(non-living) factors determine the ease of growth of a species in an ecosystem. Common factors are:

Temperature

External temperature affect all animals, especially those that do not maintain constant internal body temperatures. Extremes of temperature require special adaptations, so some species can survive them.

Water

Animals vary in the amount of water that they require. Some animals are aquatic and must have water to live in and at the other extreme some animals including desert rats are adapted to survive in an environment where they are unlikely ever to drink water.

Breeding Sites

All species of animals breed at some stage in their life cycle. Some animals require a specific site and can only exist in areas where those sites are available. For example, mosquitoes need stagnant water in which to lay their eggs.

Food Supply

Many animal species are adapted to feed on specific food types and only inhabit areas where this food source is available. For example, blue whales feed on krill and will only live in areas of the ocean where krill are abundant.

Territory

Some species of animals establish and defend territories, either for feeding or breeding. This tends to give the species an even rather than a clumped distribution. For example, tawny owls defend a single territory throughout the whole of their adult lives.

Population Sampling


Sampling is usually random.

Sampling Methods

Quadrats

Random sampling of areas(quadrats) within the territory occupied by a species.

Transect

A line sampled. Particularly useful when there is a gradient in an abiotic variable.

Biomass

Biomass is the total dry weight of organic matter of a group of organisms or an ecosystem. Obtaining this measurement is destructive, thus sample sizes are kept as small as possible.

Useful in tracking trophic levels.

Method for measuring biomass

  1. Collect a sample of all living organisms in the ecosystem – random sampling should be used.
  2. The organisms should be sorted into trophic levels
  3. The organisms are dried, by being placed in an oven at 60-800 C
  4. The mass of the organisms in each trophic level is measured using an electronic balance
  5. Drying and measuring the mass may be repeated to check that samples were completely dry.

Interactions between Species

  • Herbivory (consumption of plants)
  • Predation (consumption of animals)
  • Parasitism (living off host)
  • Competition (between organisms for resources)
  • Mutualism (mutually beneficial interactions between species.)

It is hard to perfectly classify tropic levels due to multiple sources of nutrition from different categories.

Ecological Niche

All of the biotic and abiotic resources that the organism interacts with are components of its niche.

The niche includes:

  • Habitat – where the species lives in the ecosystem
  • Nutrition – how the species obtains its food
  • Relationships – the interactions with other species in the ecosystem

The fundamental niche of a species is its potential mode of existence given the adaptations of the species.

The realized niche of a species is its actual mode of existence which results from competition from other species.

If two species have exactly the same niche, adaptation is not possible, and one of the two species will inevitably prove to be the superior competitor. This species will cause the disappearance of the other from the ecosystem.

The principle that only one species can ever occupy a niche in an ecosystem is called the competitive exclusion principle.

Biomes

Types of ecosystems. (basically)

Examples of types of ecosystems;

  1. Desert
  2. Grassland
  3. Shrubland
  4. Temperate deciduous forest
    (A forest that looses it's leaves in fall.)
  5. Tropical rainforest
  6. Tundra
    Icy cold place.

Ecological Succession

The growth and development of an environment. Abiotic factors influence biotic factors, and vice versa, as an environment develops towards a "climax", a stable state.

Common Changes in Abiotic Environment

  1. The amount of organic matter in the soil increases as organic matter released by plants and other organisms accumulates
  2. The soil becomes deeper as organic matter helps to bind mineral matter together
  3. The soil structure improves as the organic matter content rises, increasing the amount of water that can be retained and the rate at which excess water drains through.
  4. Soil erosion is reduced by the binding action of the roots of larger plants
  5. The amounts of mineral recycling increases, as the soil can hold larger amounts and more minerals are held in the increasing biomass of the community

Primary succession

Growth of an ecosystem in an environment where living organisms have not previously existed.

Secondary Succession

Regrowth in areas where an ecosystem was/is present, but is replaced by other ecosystems, because of a change in conditions.

Biodiversity

Population diversity; Within an environment, the diversity of species.
Environmental diversity; Globally, the diversity of biomes

Measuring Diversity "Diversity Index"

Method

  1. Use a random sampling technique to search for organisms in the ecosystem
  2. Identify each of the organisms found
  3. Count the total number of individuals of each species
  4. Calculate the index (D)


High D = very stable and ancient site
Low D = pollution or disturbed site

Monitoring Environmental Change

Bioindicators

Change in population density of indicator species (species that thrive in specific conditions, I.E. high/low oxygen concentration) can represent change in certain environmental conditions.

Change can be predicted over time by looking at:
1. Historical records,
2. A response to a particular stimulus in a controlled environment,
3. spatial impact e.g. using different distances from the source of sewage pollution in a river as pollution will eventually spread more over time

Indicator species can indicate a consistent environment too, hinting at primary or secondary succession.
